course outline

  • rhythm

    The root of all music. You will learn about counting in time, a crucial skill for any musician. By the end of this section, you will be able to write drum patterns in MIDI. You will be provided with a Bitwig Drum Machine preset to work with, and focus only on writing rhythms.

    • tempo

      Beyond just being a measure of speed, it is also a measure of energy. Combined with time signature, the perception of speed can be manipulated greatly, without ever changing tempo. You will explore working in different tempos, and will learn the average tempo ranges for many genres. By the end of this section, you will be able to find the tempo of any track.

      • definition

        Music is the manipulation of sounds in structured and pleasant ways over the passage of time. Therefore, we must first understand music's relationship with time. Tempo is measured is Beats Per Minute, or BPM. This is simply a count of the beats in a minute. In simpler terms, how many times you tap your foot to the rhythm in one minute.

        For example, 120 BPM is equivalent to 2 beats per second. This establishes the pace. Here are some examples of tempo, counted in 4 seconds for brevity. Multiplying the amount of beats by 15 (because 1min = 60s / 4s = 15) gives you the BPM.

      • energy
    • time signature

      Music that never repeats is not music. Time signature defines the rules and structure of your track, as well as the measure of musical time which the repetition, or loop, will be built around. By the end of this section you will be able to count any 4/4 track (most music is in 4/4), and have the understanding to explore other time signatures.

    • patterns

      You will apply the concepts of tempo and time signature through learning common drum patterns in some genres of your choice, as well as writing your own. You will also learn about breaks, which are crucial to create and release tension in your music. By the end of this section, you will have a few patterns written that are building blocks for a track.

    • velocity

      At this point your patterns will feel and sound robotic. You will learn how to humanize them through velocity. When a musician plays anything, they never hit a note or drum exactly the same twice in a row, there is always some slight variation. You will recreate this through manual velocity editing, as well a modulation based velocity variation. By the end of this section, you will have humanized the patterns you created earlier.

    • accents

      To create even more variations in your patterns, you will explore the use of accents, and how the same pattern can be represented in many different ways as you accent different notes. By the end of this section, you will have a complete set of patterns with multiple variations to build a track with.

  • melody

    While rhythm is the motion in music, melody is the voice. Music is an abstract language that conveys emotions and mind states. Learning to write melodies is the equivalent of learning to speak as a child; you will go from making noise, to expressing yourself more and more eloquently with time.

    • intervals

      Music is expressed through the relationship between notes. Your musical expression is defined by your movement through the notes, and the distances between them. This is melody at it's simplest. By the end of this section, you will have the foundational understanding necessary to begin learning scales, which represent the spectrum of musical expression.

    • scales

      This is the core foundation melody and harmony. Playing a scale is essentially limiting the notes you can play to a set of rules defined by cultures, musicians, religions, etc. all throughout time. There 24,576 possible scales, in case you were curious. By the end of this section, you will have written a melody in a Bitwig MIDI clip, using provided instrument presets.

  • harmony

    A melody, be it looping or evolving, is expression of a story or emotion, and therefore requires context. The same melody can present different nuances of expression based on its context. Harmony is what contextualizes the melody. Of course, it can also work the other way around, where melody contextualizes harmony. This is all up to the producer. By the end of this section, you will have fleshed out a melody by adding an accompanying bassline and chords.

    • bass

      The bassline is the glue between rhythm and harmony. You will learn about its relationship with each and write your own basslines to accompany your melodies. This is further solidify your understanding of melody and harmony, and circle back to the rhythm section as we begin to merge the two.

    • chords

      Chords breathe life into music, and are fundamental to any track. I can think of many tracks without rhythm, with no discerning melody, but never without chords. You will learn to build chords from your melody and vice versa. You will also learn about chord progressions, write your own, and write melodies on top of them. By the end of this section, the musical part of the course will be complete, and you will have your own set of clips to build a track with.
